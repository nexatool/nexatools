How to Convert PNG to JPG Without Losing Image Quality

📅 July 11, 2026 ⏱ 7 min read ✍️ NexaTools Team

PNG and JPG are the two most common image formats on the web, and the choice between them affects both file size and visual quality more than most people realize. If you have a folder full of PNG screenshots or graphics that are taking up far more storage than they should, converting them to JPG can shrink file sizes dramatically often by 70% or more without any noticeable difference to the eye, as long as you convert them correctly.

This guide explains how PNG and JPG actually differ under the hood, when converting from PNG to JPG makes sense, and how to do it while keeping visual quality as close to the original as possible.

PNG vs. JPG: What's Actually Different

PNG uses lossless compression, meaning it preserves every single pixel exactly as it was created, with no data thrown away. This makes PNG ideal for screenshots, logos, and graphics with sharp edges or text, since nothing gets blurred or degraded. The tradeoff is file size PNG files, especially of photographs, can be significantly larger than they need to be.

JPG uses lossy compression, which selectively discards image data that the human eye is less likely to notice, particularly in areas of subtle color variation like skies, skin tones, or shadows. This makes JPG far more efficient for photographic images, often producing files a fraction of the size of an equivalent PNG with minimal visible difference.

PNG Also Supports Transparency

One major functional difference is that PNG supports transparent backgrounds, while standard JPG does not. If your PNG image relies on transparency such as a logo meant to sit over a colored background converting it to JPG will replace the transparent areas with a solid color, usually white. Keep this in mind before converting logos or graphics intended for overlay use.

When You Should Convert PNG to JPG

  • Photographs saved as PNG photos rarely need PNG's lossless precision and convert to JPG with dramatic file size savings
  • Screenshots you plan to share or email JPG versions load faster and take up less space in messages and attachments
  • Website images smaller JPG files improve page load speed, which matters for both user experience and search rankings
  • Large batches of images for storage converting bulk PNG photo libraries to JPG can free up significant device or cloud storage

When You Should Keep PNG Instead

  • Logos and graphics with transparency converting removes the transparent background
  • Screenshots of text or UI elements PNG keeps edges of text and icons crisp; JPG can introduce slight blurring around sharp lines
  • Images that will be edited further repeatedly saving a JPG through multiple edits compounds quality loss, while PNG stays lossless through edits

How Much Smaller Will the File Actually Be?

The size reduction depends heavily on the image content. A screenshot with large blocks of solid color might shrink by 40-60%, while a detailed photograph originally saved as PNG can shrink by 80% or more when converted to a high-quality JPG. Images with lots of fine texture, like grass, fabric, or hair, tend to compress less dramatically than smoother images, but the savings are still substantial in nearly every case.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does converting PNG to JPG reduce quality?

JPG uses lossy compression, so there is technically some data loss, but at quality settings of 85% or higher, the difference is generally invisible to the naked eye for most photographic content.

Can I convert a JPG back to PNG later?

Yes, but doing so will not restore any detail lost during the original PNG-to-JPG conversion. Converting back to PNG only changes the file format going forward, not the image data already discarded.

Why is my PNG file so much larger than a similar JPG?

PNG's lossless compression preserves every pixel exactly, which naturally results in larger files, especially for photographs. JPG's lossy compression strips out data the eye is unlikely to notice, resulting in much smaller files.

Will converting to JPG remove transparency from my image?

Yes. Standard JPG does not support transparency, so any transparent areas in a PNG will be filled with a solid background color, typically white, after conversion.
